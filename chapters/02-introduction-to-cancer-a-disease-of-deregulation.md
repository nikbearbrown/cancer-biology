# Introduction to Cancer: A Disease of Deregulation

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter you should be able to:

- **Explain** why cancer is best understood as a category of diseases unified by deregulated cell behavior rather than as one disease with one cause.
- **Describe** the hallmarks framework as a set of acquired capabilities, and map a specific tumor behavior onto the hallmark mechanism that produces it.
- **Distinguish** benign from malignant, and grade from stage, and predict what each descriptor tells you about prognosis and treatment.
- **Apply** the TNM staging system and the carcinoma/sarcoma/leukemia/lymphoma taxonomy to classify a clinical scenario.

## Opening Case

A pathology report comes back on a 58-year-old woman with a breast lump. It uses four words she does not know: *invasive ductal carcinoma, grade 3, stage IIA, ER-positive*. Her sister, ten years earlier, had a *grade 1, stage IIIB* tumor and did badly. The patient assumes a "grade 3" must be worse than her sister's "grade 1" — higher number, worse disease.

She is reading the wrong descriptor. Grade and stage measure different things, and the one that drives prognosis here is stage, not grade. Her sister's lower-grade tumor had spread further (stage IIIB), which mattered more than how the cells looked under the microscope. Meanwhile the same report contains words — *carcinoma*, *invasive* — that encode a deep biological claim about what these cells can now do that normal cells cannot: divide without an external signal, ignore the brakes that should stop them, refuse to die, and breach the boundary that should contain them.

Before this vocabulary means anything, you need the underlying idea: cancer is not a foreign invader. It is your own cells, descended from your own DNA, that have lost the regulatory contract holding multicellular tissue together (NCI, *What Is Cancer?*). This chapter builds the framework — the hallmarks of what cancer cells can do, and the clinical vocabulary that translates that biology into a pathology report.

## Core Concepts

### Cancer as deregulation, not invasion

Most diseases medicine has conquered were caused by something foreign — a bacterium, a virus, a toxin you can name, isolate, and target precisely *because* it is not you. Cancer breaks that strategy. Tumor cells carry your DNA, your machinery, your membrane proteins. What has changed is a small number of letters in a few critical genes, and those changes have unhooked one lineage from the regulatory system described in Chapter 1. That is why therapy is hard: killing the cancer cell well tends to damage the normal cells it resembles. The art of oncology is the search for **therapeutic index** — the gap between the dose that kills the cancer and the dose that kills the patient.

A common student instinct is to ask for "the cause of cancer," as though it were one disease. It is not. The NCI defines cancer functionally: cells that grow when they should not, ignore signals to stop or die, and can spread (NCI, *What Is Cancer?*). That functional definition is the key move — it tells you cancer is unified by *what cells do*, not by which mutation started it.

### The hallmarks framework

In 2000, Douglas Hanahan and Robert Weinberg asked: regardless of which mutations cause it, what must *every* cancer cell be able to do? Their answer was a finite list of acquired capabilities — a teaching scaffold that organized a field drowning in molecular particulars (Hanahan, 2022, reviewing the framework's evolution). The point worth holding: hallmarks are *mechanisms*, not labels to memorize, and they are not a checklist every tumor satisfies in the same order.

The original six capabilities:

- **Sustaining proliferative signaling.** Normal cells divide only on receiving an external growth signal; cancer cells manufacture their own or lock their receptors "on." (This is the RAS/MAPK pathway of Chapter 1, stuck active.)
- **Evading growth suppressors.** Cancer cells lose the brakes — the tumor suppressors. *TP53* is mutated in roughly half of human cancers, *RB1* and *APC* in others.
- **Resisting cell death.** The apoptosis program is silenced, often by overexpressing Bcl-2 or losing p53's death-inducing capacity (Letai, 2016).
- **Enabling replicative immortality.** Normal cells count divisions via shortening telomeres and stop after roughly fifty (the Hayflick limit); cancer cells reactivate telomerase and reset the counter.
- **Inducing angiogenesis.** A tumor over about 1 mm cannot survive on diffusion; it secretes factors (chiefly VEGF) to recruit blood vessels.
- **Activating invasion and metastasis.** Cells detach, enter circulation, and seed distant organs — the capability that converts a curable disease into an incurable one.

In 2011, two **emerging hallmarks** — reprogramming energy metabolism (the Warburg effect) and evading immune destruction — and two **enabling characteristics** — genome instability and mutation, and tumor-promoting inflammation — were added. In 2022, Hanahan added four further dimensions: **unlocking phenotypic plasticity** (cells de-differentiating into new states), **non-mutational epigenetic reprogramming** (chromatin changes without DNA-sequence changes), **polymorphic microbiomes**, and **senescent cells** (Hanahan, 2022). The 2022 additions are presented honestly as active synthesis, not settled dogma — the status of plasticity, microbiome effects, and senescence as stable hallmarks is still debated [contested — see pantry flag].

The lesson of the framework is durable: twenty-two years on, it is still a finite list of capabilities a cancer cell must acquire. Each chapter of this book traces one of them.

### Benign and malignant

A **benign** tumor is cells dividing more than they should but staying put — a lipoma (a benign fat-cell growth) gets bigger, does not invade, does not spread, and does not recur if fully removed. It can still kill if positioned badly (a benign meningioma against the brainstem), but the cells lack the capability to escape. A **malignant** tumor — what people usually mean by "cancer" — invades surrounding tissue and can seed distant organs. The line is not always crisp: a colorectal adenomatous polyp is benign but carries an *APC* mutation and can, over a decade or two, accumulate enough further mutations to become carcinoma (NCBI Bookshelf, *Development and Causes of Cancer*). That window is exactly what colonoscopy exploits.

Cancer is also classified by tissue of origin. **Carcinomas** arise from epithelial cells (skin, gut lining, breast, prostate) and account for 85–90% of human cancers; *adenocarcinomas* come from glandular epithelium, *squamous cell carcinomas* from flat surface epithelium. **Sarcomas** arise from connective tissue (bone, muscle, fat) and are rare in adults but disproportionately common in children. **Leukemias and lymphomas** arise from blood-forming and immune lineages. **CNS tumors** and **germ cell tumors** form their own categories. The taxonomy is not pure — small-cell and non-small-cell lung carcinoma share an organ but are different diseases — but it tells you, on first encounter, where to look.

### Grade and stage

These two words sound alike and mean different things — the heart of the opening case.

**Grade** describes how the tumor cells *look* under the microscope: how much they still resemble the tissue they came from. A well-differentiated (low-grade) tumor still looks recognizably like its parent tissue; a poorly differentiated (high-grade) tumor has lost that identity — enlarged irregular nuclei, no organized structure — and generally behaves more aggressively.

**Stage** describes how far the tumor has *spread*, and it drives treatment more than anything else. The standard system is **TNM**: **T** (tumor size and local invasion, T1–T4), **N** (lymph node involvement, N0–N3), **M** (distant metastasis, M0 or M1). These combine into stage groupings 0 through IV. Five-year survival drops steeply with stage across nearly every cancer — for colorectal cancer roughly 90% at stage I versus about 14% at stage IV (ACS, *Cancer Facts & Figures 2026*). This steep gradient is why **screening** exists: a colonoscopy that removes a stage 0 adenoma prevents a stage III cancer years later. Screening carries a trade-off — **overdiagnosis** of indolent tumors that would never have killed the patient, the canonical worry with PSA testing for prostate cancer — so screening design is a real public-health optimization, not a free good.

### The numbers

Globally in 2022 there were approximately 20 million new cancer diagnoses and 9.7 million cancer deaths, the most recent comprehensive GLOBOCAN estimates (IARC, 2024). The top cancers by incidence were lung, breast, colorectal, prostate, and stomach; lung was the leading cause of cancer death (about 1.8 million). In the United States, recent annual figures run around 2 million new cases and roughly 600,000 deaths, making cancer the second leading cause of death after heart disease (ACS, 2026). About 13% of cancers worldwide are attributable to infectious agents, concentrated in lower-income regions (IARC, 2024). Cancer is overwhelmingly a disease of age — about half of diagnoses occur after 65 — and outcomes are shaped heavily by access, not biology alone, a theme Chapter 3 develops.

## Worked Example

**Situation.** Classify three growths against the functional definition of cancer: (a) a subcutaneous lipoma; (b) a breast lesion called *ductal carcinoma in situ* (DCIS); (c) a breast carcinoma that has spread to bone.

**Analytical process.** Start with the wrong shortcut: "Any abnormal growth is cancer." That fails immediately on the lipoma. The lipoma's cells divide more than normal fat cells, but they stay put, do not invade, do not metastasize. They satisfy "uncontrolled growth" only weakly and fail "invasion/spread" entirely — so by the functional definition (NCI, *What Is Cancer?*) the lipoma is benign, not cancer.

Now DCIS. The word *carcinoma* signals malignant-type cells, but *in situ* means they have not breached the basement membrane. The cells have acquired several hallmarks — sustained proliferation, evaded brakes — but not yet invasion. This is the genuinely ambiguous middle: stage 0 disease, treated to prevent progression, and a site of real overdiagnosis debate, because not every DCIS would become invasive.

The bone lesion is the clear case. Cells from the breast have invaded, entered circulation, survived, and colonized a distant organ — the full set of capabilities including metastasis. Topologically it sits in bone, but genetically and behaviorally it is breast cancer, and it is staged M1, stage IV.

**Resolution.** Three growths, three positions on the deregulation spectrum: benign, in-situ/borderline, fully malignant. The functional definition — not the location or the cell type alone — sorts them.

**The lesson.** "Cancer" is defined by acquired *capabilities*, not by being an abnormal lump; the same word covers a benign-adjacent in-situ lesion and a lethal metastatic one only by tracking which hallmarks each has acquired.

**The limit.** The hallmark capabilities are not binary switches you can read off a single slide. DCIS shows the boundary is fuzzy: capabilities accumulate continuously, and predicting which in-situ lesion will turn invasive remains imperfect.

## Common Misconceptions

**"There is a single cause of cancer."** Cancer is a category of diseases sharing a structural failure, not one disease. A BRCA-mutated breast tumor, an EGFR-mutated lung tumor, and a Philadelphia-chromosome leukemia look nothing alike molecularly but have each acquired some subset of the same capabilities. Asking for "the cause" mistakes a family for an individual — and, as the opening case shows, the patient's tumor and her sister's are both "breast cancer" yet biologically distinct.

**"The hallmarks are a checklist every tumor ticks in order."** They are acquired capabilities, reached by different routes in different orders. Some tumors are driven by a few dominant changes, others by many small ones. Treating the hallmarks as a fixed sequence misreads them as bookkeeping rather than mechanism (Hanahan, 2022).

**"Higher grade always means worse prognosis."** Grade describes cell appearance; stage describes spread — and stage usually dominates prognosis. The opening-case patient's grade-3 stage-IIA tumor can carry a better outlook than her sister's grade-1 stage-IIIB tumor, because the further-spread tumor is harder to cure regardless of how its cells look.

**"Benign means harmless."** Benign means non-invasive and non-metastatic, not harmless. A benign tumor in a critical location can be lethal by mass effect; and some benign lesions carry mutations that put them en route to malignancy.

## What Would Change My Mind

The chapter's central claim is that cancer is unified by deregulated *behavior* — a finite set of acquired capabilities — rather than by any shared cause or molecule. A finding that would force revision: a large, well-characterized cancer type that achieves uncontrolled growth, death resistance, and spread *without* acquiring the hallmark capabilities — for instance, a malignant, metastatic tumor driven entirely by a mechanism orthogonal to all the cataloged hallmarks and not reducible to any of them. The hallmarks framework has absorbed surprises by *adding* dimensions (2011, 2022) rather than being overturned; a cancer that fits none of them, even after expansion, would mean the unifying-capabilities frame is incomplete in kind, not just in detail.

## Still Puzzling

- Are the 2022 additions — plasticity, microbiomes, senescence — genuine hallmarks on par with the original six, or context-dependent modifiers? The field has not settled this [contested — see pantry flag].
- Why do some in-situ lesions (DCIS) progress to invasive cancer while others never would? Predicting progression is exactly where the clean hallmark logic gets blurry, and it drives the overdiagnosis problem.
- How much of cancer's clinical heterogeneity reflects different *routes* to the same capabilities versus genuinely different end-states? This bears directly on whether one therapeutic strategy can ever generalize.
