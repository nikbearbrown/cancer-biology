# Chapter 9 — Cancer Etiology: Chemical and Radiation Carcinogens
*How a molecule from smoke, or a photon of sunlight, changes one DNA letter — and how a tumor genome read today can name the exposure from decades ago.*

The tumor sequencing report arrives and the oncologist looks at the *TP53* gene. There is a guanine-to-thymine change at a familiar position. Scattered across the rest of the genome: the same kind of change, again and again, concentrated at specific sequence contexts. G→T, G→T, G→T. The oncologist has seen this pattern before. So has anyone who reads these reports for lung adenocarcinomas in patients with heavy smoking histories.

That pattern is not noise. It is a fingerprint, pressed into this man's DNA by a molecule from cigarette smoke — a molecule his own enzymes converted into something reactive — that bound covalently to a guanine base thirty or forty years ago. When the cell next copied that DNA, the polymerase misread the damaged site and inserted the wrong nucleotide. One letter changed. The change happened in a critical gene. Eventually, after decades of continued exposure, continued replication, and the gradual accumulation of other changes, a cancer formed. The tumor genome today is a record of a chemistry lesson that happened in an airway cell before most of the doctors reading the report were born.

This chapter is about that chemistry lesson. How does a molecule change a DNA letter? How does a photon do it differently? And how do the resulting patterns make it possible to read a tumor genome as a history of what the cell encountered?

---

### Carcinogens act by making mutations

The unifying principle is simple enough to state in a sentence: most chemical and radiation carcinogens cause cancer by introducing mutations — heritable changes in the DNA sequence — and if those mutations land in the right genes, a cell gains a selective growth advantage and begins the slow march toward malignancy.

"Simple to state" does not mean "all at once." The path from a first carcinogen exposure to a diagnosed cancer is typically measured in decades. The carcinogen introduces a mutation in one cell. That cell sits in the tissue, indistinguishable from its neighbors, for years. Something causes it to proliferate preferentially — chronic inflammation, a hormonal environment, repeated cycles of tissue injury and repair. The expanded clone accumulates further mutations. Eventually a combination of changes sufficient for invasive cancer emerges. The time scale from first exposure to diagnosis in heavy smokers is typically 20–40 years. The man in the opening case smoked for 35 years before the lung mass appeared.

This delay is not a defect in the mechanism. It is a feature of the biology: cancer requires not one event but many, in the right sequence, in the right cell. Understanding the delay requires the three-stage framework the field has used since the 1940s.

---

### Initiation, promotion, progression

The experimental system that built this framework was mouse skin. A group working in the mid-twentieth century found something that should have been puzzling. A single topical application of a genotoxic chemical — dimethylbenz[a]anthracene, a polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon — to mouse skin produced no tumors, even if the mice were watched for their entire lives. The chemical entered the skin, damaged some DNA, and apparently nothing happened.

Then researchers tried a different sequence. Apply the genotoxic chemical once. Wait. Then apply, repeatedly, a completely different compound — a phorbol ester called TPA — that has no direct DNA-damaging activity, no mutagenic action in standard assays, and produces no tumors on its own. The mice developed skin tumors. Reverse the order — TPA first, then the genotoxic compound — and nothing happened.

The result defines three stages. **Initiation** is the irreversible step: a carcinogen introduces a mutation in a target cell. The initiated cell carries altered DNA but is phenotypically normal, clinically invisible, dormant. **Promotion** is the typically reversible step: non-mutagenic factors — the phorbol ester in the skin model, chronic inflammation in the liver, estrogen stimulation in the breast — cause the initiated cell to proliferate selectively, expanding its clone and giving subsequent mutations more chances to arise. **Progression** is the further accumulation of changes that finally yield invasive, metastatic cancer.

The mouse-skin result encodes a lesson about prevention: you can intervene at either stage. Remove the initiator and no cells receive the founding mutation. Remove the promoter and initiated cells remain dormant. The two strategies are not equivalent in every context — an exposure that has already initiated many cells needs promoter removal to matter — but both are real handles on the disease.

Real carcinogenesis rarely cleaves this neatly into two-agent systems. Most carcinogens have both initiating and promoting activity, acting on the same cell. And the promotion phase in human cancer is often decades of chronic inflammation or hormonal stimulation rather than a discrete compound. But the framework does real work, and the aflatoxin-hepatitis B story that closes this chapter is a case where the two-stage structure is unusually legible.

### Chemical carcinogens: the metabolic paradox

Most chemical carcinogens are not reactive as ingested or inhaled. They are **procarcinogens** — stable molecules that must be chemically converted by the cell before they can damage DNA. The enzymes that do this conversion, primarily the **cytochrome P450** family, exist not to cause cancer but to detoxify foreign compounds: they oxidize lipophilic molecules into more water-soluble derivatives that can be conjugated and excreted. For most compounds, this detoxification is protective. For a subset, it creates the problem: the oxidized intermediate is more reactive than the parent compound, and if it escapes conjugation, it attacks DNA.

**Benzo[a]pyrene** is the canonical case. A polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon produced by incomplete combustion — in cigarette smoke, charred meat, diesel exhaust, coal tar — benzo[a]pyrene is inert as inhaled. Cytochrome P450 enzymes, particularly CYP1A1 and CYP1B1, convert it in two steps to benzo[a]pyrene diol epoxide (**BPDE**). BPDE is the **ultimate carcinogen** — the reactive form that does the damage. It carries a strained epoxide ring that opens and binds covalently to the N2 position of guanine, forming a bulky **DNA adduct**: a chemical group hanging off the base that distorts the double helix.

When DNA polymerase encounters this adduct during replication, it cannot accurately read through it. The most common misreading inserts adenine opposite the damaged guanine — a mismatch that, after one more round of replication, becomes a fixed **G→T transversion** in the sequence. Repeated across many cells over decades of tobacco exposure, this chemistry produces the mutational signature the oncologist recognized in the opening case: an excess of G→T transversions at specific sequence contexts, concentrated at codons in *TP53* that are repeatedly hit in lung cancers from smokers.

The BPDE story is one instance of a general pattern. **N-nitrosamines** in tobacco smoke alkylate oxygen atoms in guanine and cytosine, producing a different set of adducts and a different mutational signature. **Aflatoxin B1** forms an epoxide that binds guanine N7 and produces a characteristic G→T transversion at a specific codon in *TP53* with near-perfect specificity. **Benzene** is metabolized to benzene oxide, which forms adducts with multiple bases and also generates reactive oxygen species. Each carcinogen traces a specific chemical path through the same basic sequence: metabolic activation, adduct formation, replication error, mutation. What differs is the chemistry at each step — and that specificity is what makes mutational signatures readable.

### Reading the signature

A **mutational signature** is the characteristic spectrum of base changes — and their surrounding sequence contexts — left across a genome by a specific mutagenic process. The concept became tractable when large-scale tumor sequencing, cataloguing millions of mutations across thousands of tumors, revealed that many tumors carry recognizable patterns. The field organized these into a reference catalogue (COSMIC Mutational Signatures, Alexandrov et al., *Nature*, 2013): Signature 4 is the tobacco smoking signature (C→A at specific contexts, reflecting G→T on the complementary strand); Signature 7 is the UV signature; Signature 22 is aflatoxin; Signature 6 is mismatch-repair deficiency. Each signature is, in effect, a chemical fingerprint pressed into the genome.

Reading a signature in a tumor tells you something about the cell's history that the clinical record may not contain. The aflatoxin signature concentrated at *TP53* codon 249 (the R249S mutation) is so specific — occurring in hepatocellular carcinomas from regions of high aflatoxin contamination and rarely elsewhere — that it is considered diagnostic for that exposure. The UV signature of C→T transitions at dipyrimidine sites is present in essentially every melanoma and squamous cell carcinoma of sun-exposed skin. The APOBEC signatures (Signatures 2 and 13) appear in cervical cancer and many other tumor types and reflect the activity of a family of cytidine deaminases that are activated by viral infection and other stresses.

The practical value is double. First, the signature can suggest an exposure where the clinical history is incomplete or unavailable — a lung cancer in a never-smoker with a tobacco signature might reveal secondary smoke exposure. Second, signatures left by endogenous processes — errors from the normal activity of replication polymerases, spontaneous deamination of methylcytosine — tell you what the background mutation rate looks like without exogenous carcinogen, which is essential for interpreting whether an observed exposure-associated rate is genuinely elevated above baseline.

### Ionizing radiation: the physics of strand breaking

Ionizing radiation — X-rays, gamma rays, alpha particles, beta particles, neutrons — carries enough energy to eject electrons from atoms, breaking chemical bonds. When it passes through tissue, it damages DNA both directly (ionizing DNA atoms) and indirectly (ionizing water molecules to produce hydroxyl radicals, which diffuse nanometers to the DNA and attack it). Its most consequential lesion is the **double-strand break**: both DNA strands severed at or near the same position.

Double-strand breaks are dangerous not because they cannot be repaired — they can, by homologous recombination and non-homologous end-joining — but because the repair is imprecise. Non-homologous end-joining, the dominant pathway in post-replicative cells, simply ligates the broken ends, which are often trimmed or extended before joining. If the break was caused by a passing ionizing particle that also broke a nearby chromosome, the wrong ends can be joined. This produces **chromosomal translocations, inversions, and deletions** — the rearrangements that are characteristic of radiation-associated cancers. The leukemias and thyroid cancers elevated in atomic-bomb survivors carry chromosomal rearrangements consistent with misrepaired double-strand breaks, not the point mutations typical of chemical carcinogens.

The dose-response model for ionizing radiation — the regulatory standard for setting permissible exposures — assumes no threshold: every dose, however small, carries proportional risk. This **linear no-threshold (LNT) model** is supported by the atomic-bomb survivor Life Span Study, which shows cancer risk rising roughly linearly with dose from 100 mSv down toward zero, with no clear threshold below which risk disappears. The model is contested at very low doses, where statistical power is insufficient to rule out hormetic effects (protective at low dose) or a genuine threshold. The LNT model is a regulatory assumption, not a firmly established biological fact at doses below about 50 mSv — but it is a defensible and conservative one.

**Radon**, a naturally occurring radioactive gas produced by uranium decay in soil and rock, accumulates in poorly ventilated spaces, particularly basements. When inhaled, its decay products emit alpha particles directly in the lung epithelium. Alpha particles, despite being stopped by a sheet of paper, deposit their energy densely along short tracks — highly effective at producing double-strand breaks and chromosome rearrangements in the cells they traverse. Radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer after tobacco in the United States, responsible for an estimated 21,000 deaths annually.

### Ultraviolet radiation: the chemistry of dimer formation

UV radiation is the point where a common misconception needs to be corrected before it takes root. UV-B photons (280–315 nm) are not ionizing. They cannot eject electrons from atoms; they do not break chemical bonds by direct ionization; they do not produce double-strand breaks. Their mechanism is entirely different.

When a UV-B photon is absorbed by adjacent pyrimidine bases on the same DNA strand — two thymines, or a thymine and a cytosine — it drives covalent bonding between the 5,6 positions of the two rings, forming a **cyclobutane pyrimidine dimer**. The dimer distorts the helix. It stalls the replication machinery. Translesion synthesis polymerases that can copy past the stall tend to insert adenine opposite both residues of the dimer, which means the downstream mutation is typically **C→T or CC→TT** at the site of the dimer — specifically at dipyrimidine sequence contexts.

This C→T transition at dipyrimidine sites is the UV mutational signature, and it is unmistakable in melanomas and squamous cell carcinomas of sun-exposed skin. The double substitution CC→TT, while rare, is almost diagnostic of UV exposure; it is extremely uncommon from any other mutagenic process. The geographical correlation is exactly what you would expect from photon exposure: melanoma rates rise closer to the equator and at higher altitude, where UV intensity is greater; they are elevated on sun-exposed body surfaces; they are reduced in populations with more melanin, which absorbs UV before it reaches the nuclear DNA. Tanning beds, which emit UV at intensities comparable to the midday sun at low latitudes, are IARC Group 1 carcinogens for melanoma.

The cell's defense is **nucleotide excision repair (NER)**, a multi-protein pathway that recognizes helical distortions, excises a short oligonucleotide containing the dimer, and fills the gap with the complementary strand as template. The genetic disease **xeroderma pigmentosum** results from inherited defects in NER components — and affected individuals develop sun-induced skin cancers in childhood at thousands of times the normal rate, demonstrating the repair pathway's importance with terrible clarity.

### Hazard is not risk

Every discussion of carcinogens eventually encounters the IARC classification, and almost every public encounter with it involves the same misreading. The system has four groups: **Group 1** (carcinogenic to humans), **2A** (probably carcinogenic), **2B** (possibly carcinogenic), and **3** (not classifiable). Tobacco smoke is Group 1. Processed meat is Group 1. Red meat is Group 2A. Glyphosate is Group 2A. Night-shift work is Group 2A.

The system classifies **hazard**: the strength of evidence that an agent *can cause cancer under some exposure conditions in some population*. It says nothing about how large the risk is at typical real-world exposure levels. Tobacco smoke and processed meat share the Group 1 designation and have nothing else in common about magnitude of risk. A heavy smoker's lifetime excess cancer risk from tobacco is enormous; the estimated excess colorectal cancer risk from eating two slices of bacon daily is on the order of a few percent absolute increase. Both are real. They are not comparable.

This distinction — hazard versus risk — is why the IARC system should never be used to rank carcinogens by danger. It was not designed to do that. Its purpose is to determine whether the evidence supports causation at all. Once Group 1 is established, the magnitude question is answered by dose-response analysis and quantitative risk assessment, which are separate exercises. A Group 1 label in a news headline is evidence that the science supports causation; it is not evidence that the exposure is as dangerous as any other Group 1 agent at typical doses.

### Aflatoxin and hepatitis B: reading the two-stage model in a real epidemic

The hepatocellular carcinoma burden in parts of sub-Saharan Africa and East Asia is among the highest for any cancer type in any region. Two carcinogenic exposures correlate with it: aflatoxin B1, from *Aspergillus* molds contaminating stored grain and groundnuts in humid conditions; and chronic hepatitis B infection. The question is which one drives the excess, and the answer illuminates both mutational signatures and the initiation-promotion framework.

Aflatoxin B1 is a procarcinogen. Cytochrome P450 converts it to an 8,9-epoxide that attacks guanine N7, producing an adduct that depurinates and then causes a G→T transversion. The specificity is striking: the resulting mutation at *TP53* codon 249 (arginine to serine, R249S) is found in hepatocellular carcinomas from high-aflatoxin regions at frequencies of 30–50 percent and in hepatocellular carcinomas from low-aflatoxin regions at frequencies below 5 percent. The signature names the exposure.

But when researchers compared liver cancer rates across regions, the picture was not simply: more aflatoxin, more cancer. Regions with high aflatoxin but low hepatitis B prevalence had substantially lower rates than regions with both. The additive model — aflatoxin exposure plus HBV exposure, summed independently — underpredicted the excess in dual-exposure populations. Epidemiological studies estimated a greater-than-multiplicative interaction: the risk in people with both exposures exceeded the product of the risks from each alone.

The two-stage framework makes sense of this. Aflatoxin acts as an **initiator**: it introduces the R249S mutation in hepatocytes. Alone, that initiated cell sits in a liver that is otherwise normal. Hepatitis B acts as a **promoter**: the immune-mediated inflammation of chronic HBV infection drives continuous cycles of hepatocyte death and regeneration, expanding any clone that has a slight survival advantage, including the one carrying the aflatoxin-induced mutation. Together they compress the timeline and amplify the risk.

The intervention logic follows directly. To reduce the burden most efficiently in a dual-exposure region: improve grain storage and processing to reduce aflatoxin contamination (remove the initiator), and implement universal hepatitis B vaccination (remove the promoter). Both handles are real; their combined effect is likely more than additive.

3. *(Recall — difficulty: low)* What is the difference between a carcinogenic hazard and a carcinogenic risk? **Application**

4. *(Apply — difficulty: medium)* A patient with xeroderma pigmentosum develops multiple squamous cell carcinomas on sun-exposed skin by age 12. Explain mechanistically why NER deficiency produces this phenotype, trace the sequence from photon absorption to fixed mutation, and state what the mutational signature in these tumors would look like and why. *What this tests: the UV dimer mechanism and NER's protective role, with the genetic disease as a natural experiment.*

5. *(Apply — difficulty: medium)* A non-smoker develops lung adenocarcinoma. Tumor sequencing shows an APOBEC mutational signature (C→T and C→G mutations at TCA/TCT contexts) rather than the tobacco G→T signature. What does this imply about the etiology of this particular tumor? What biological process generates the APOBEC signature, and what does its presence suggest about what was happening in this lung cell? *What this tests: signature-reading as exposure history and the existence of endogenous mutagenic processes beyond exogenous carcinogens.*

6. *(Apply — difficulty: medium)* A news headline reads: "WHO declares red meat possibly carcinogenic — same agency that declared tobacco a carcinogen." Write one paragraph explaining what the headline gets right and what it misleads. Use the hazard-versus-risk distinction and the IARC group definitions precisely. State what additional information a reader would need to assess the personal risk from red meat consumption. *What this tests: systematic application of the hazard/risk distinction to a real public communication failure.*

**Synthesis**

7. *(Synthesize — difficulty: high)* Construct a complete mechanism map for aflatoxin B1-induced hepatocellular carcinoma in a patient with chronic hepatitis B infection. Then connect the two pathways to show the synergistic interaction and predict what you would observe in the mutational signature of the resulting tumor. *What this tests: integration of chemical carcinogenesis mechanism with the two-stage framework and the signature concept.*

8. *(Synthesize — difficulty: high)* Compare the mechanisms of DNA damage from UV-B radiation and from ionizing radiation at every level: the physics of energy deposition, the primary DNA lesion, the repair pathway engaged, and the characteristic cancer type.
