## A. Conceptual foundations

### Apoptotic morphology and caspases

Apoptosis is organized cellular dismantling: cell shrinkage, chromatin condensation, blebbing, apoptotic bodies, phagocytosis, and caspase-mediated cleavage. This contrasts with necrotic rupture and inflammation.

**Common misconception:** Students use apoptosis as a synonym for any cell death.

**Worked example:** Microscopy comparison: apoptotic bodies versus swollen ruptured necrotic cell.

**Source(s):** See chapter source bank below; especially the NCI/Nature/NCBI entries tagged to this chapter.

### Intrinsic mitochondrial pathway

Internal stress activates BH3-only proteins, shifts the BCL-2 family balance, enables BAX/BAK mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization, releases cytochrome c, and activates caspases.

**Common misconception:** Students memorize BCL-2 as a single molecule rather than a family balance.

**Worked example:** DNA damage causing p53-dependent activation of pro-apoptotic signals.

**Source(s):** See chapter source bank below; especially the NCI/Nature/NCBI entries tagged to this chapter.

### Extrinsic pathway and alternative death

Death receptors can trigger caspase cascades from outside the cell; necroptosis, pyroptosis, and ferroptosis are regulated death programs with growing cancer relevance.

**Common misconception:** Students assume all programmed death is apoptosis.

**Worked example:** Compare Fas death-receptor signaling with mitochondrial apoptosis.

**Source(s):** See chapter source bank below; especially the NCI/Nature/NCBI entries tagged to this chapter.

## D. Current state of the field

**Settled:**
- Cancer is best taught as a mechanism-rich family of diseases rather than one disease entity.
- Genetic, epigenetic, environmental, infectious, cell-cycle, apoptotic, and metabolic mechanisms interact rather than forming isolated chapters.
- Authoritative public-facing sources such as NCI are reliable for baseline definitions; primary reviews are needed for mechanistic depth.

**Contested or emerging:**
- The exact status of newer hallmarks and enabling characteristics, especially nonmutational epigenetic reprogramming, phenotypic plasticity, microbiome effects, and senescent cells, should be presented as active synthesis rather than settled dogma.
- Therapeutic targeting of metabolism and epigenetic state remains context-dependent; avoid implying that every pathway vulnerability has already become a general clinical tool.

**Recent developments to acknowledge:**
- The 2022 hallmarks update expanded the conceptual map beyond the 2000/2011 framework.
- Recent reviews increasingly emphasize heterogeneity, tumor microenvironment context, immune-metabolic interactions, and non-genetic plasticity.
- Current statistics and risk-factor sections should be checked against the latest ACS/NCI/IARC updates at production time.

## E. Teaching considerations

**Where students get stuck:**
- They memorize labels without causal sequence. Force every term into a sentence of the form: "When X fails, Y changes, which lets the cell/tissue do Z."
- They overgeneralize examples. Every case should include a boundary statement: where it applies, where it does not, and what evidence supports it.

**Analogies and framings that work:**
- Regulation before failure: show normal control first, then show how cancer breaks it.
- Accelerator/brake/ecosystem/factory analogies are useful if immediately tied back to molecules and evidence.

**Exercises that build the target skill:**
- Mechanism map: draw normal pathway → cancer alteration → phenotype → possible intervention or measurement.
- Misconception correction: give a false overgeneralized claim and ask students to repair it.
- Evidence sorting: classify a claim as definition, mechanism, epidemiological association, causal claim, or therapeutic implication.
