# Chapter 2 — Introduction to Cancer: A Disease of Deregulation


## TL;DR

- This chapter gives a working overview of Introduction to Cancer: A Disease of Deregulation, focusing on the ideas a reader needs before moving to the next chapter.
- The chapter moves through The historical arc, The hallmarks, Benign and malignant, Grade and stage, and related ideas.
- Read it for the main argument, the vocabulary it introduces, and the practical judgment it asks you to develop.

Cancer is not a foreign invader. It is your own body, refusing to stop.

Hold that. Most of the diseases medicine has learned to fight are caused by something coming in from outside — a bacterium, a virus, a parasite, a toxin. You can name the invader, isolate it in a dish, develop an antibiotic or a vaccine, and the invader retreats. The strategy works because the invader is *not you*. Anything that distinguishes it from your own cells is a target.

Cancer breaks the strategy. The cells that form a tumor are descended from your own cells. They carry the same DNA backbone, the same machinery, the same membrane proteins. They are running on the same biology that built you in utero and maintains you now. What has changed is a small number of letters in a few critical genes — and those changes have unhooked one lineage from the regulatory contract that makes multicellular life possible.

That is why cancer is hard. That is why chemotherapy is brutal. That is why a hundred and fifty years of serious research has produced steady, incremental progress rather than the dramatic single-shot cures that worked for polio or smallpox or diphtheria. We are not fighting a foreign thing. We are trying to talk one of our own lineages back into the contract — or, failing that, to kill it without killing the cells around it that look almost exactly the same.

This chapter does four things. It tells you how the idea of cancer evolved over five thousand years, from Egyptian papyri to the molecular era. It names the hallmarks that distinguish cancer cells from their normal neighbors — the framework Douglas Hanahan and Robert Weinberg articulated in 2000 and have updated twice since. It teaches you the vocabulary the rest of this book uses without translation: *benign*, *malignant*, *carcinoma*, *sarcoma*, *metastasis*, *grade*, *stage*. And it shows you the numbers — how common cancer is, how it kills, who it kills, why.

By the end you should be able to look at the word "cancer" and not see one disease, but a category of diseases that share a particular structural failure. Everything that follows in this book is about the mechanisms underneath that failure.

## The historical arc

The oldest written description of cancer is on a piece of papyrus. The Edwin Smith Surgical Papyrus, copied around 1600 BCE from an original perhaps a thousand years older, describes eight cases of breast tumors. Cauterization with a fire drill is the recommended treatment for most ailments. For breast tumors, the papyrus is more honest: *there is no treatment*. Whoever wrote that was watching tumors that did not respond to anything they could do, and naming the limit of medicine as they understood it. Three and a half thousand years later, for many advanced cancers, that sentence is still partially true.

Hippocrates, around 400 BCE, looked at the swollen blood vessels surrounding solid tumors and saw something crab-like — a central mass with extensions reaching out like crab legs. He coined *karkinos*, the Greek for crab. Galen, four centuries later, Latinized it to *cancer*. The metaphor stuck because it was good. A tumor really does send out invasive projections. A crab really does hold on with claws that resist pulling. You can hear the image of a crab dug into flesh every time someone says the word.

Galen also added a theory, less useful. He believed cancer arose from an excess of *black bile*, one of the four humors. The black bile theory dominated Western medicine for fifteen hundred years. It implied that cancer was systemic — a whole-body imbalance — and that surgery was therefore pointless. You can hear how that conclusion follows logically from the premise. The premise was wrong, but the inference from it was honest.

The break came with anatomy. Andreas Vesalius, the Flemish physician working in Padua in the 1540s, dissected human cadavers and produced *De humani corporis fabrica* — beautifully detailed plates of the actual structures inside a body. He could not find black bile. Anywhere. Generations of physicians had been treating an imbalance of a substance Vesalius now reported he had never seen. The humoral theory began to crack, slowly, over centuries.

By the nineteenth century, the microscope had made cells visible. In 1838, Theodor Schwann and Matthias Schleiden articulated the cell theory: all living tissues are made of cells. In 1858, Rudolf Virchow added the third clause: *omnis cellula e cellula* — every cell comes from another cell. Virchow then turned the principle on disease. Cancer, he proposed, is a disease of cells. Not of humors, not of constitutions, not of vapors. Of cells dividing when they should not.

That single reframing — *cancer is a cellular disease* — is the foundation everything since has been built on. It is the move that lets you think about cancer mechanistically. It tells you where to look (in the cells), what to ask (what is different about them), and what to measure (their behavior, their division rate, their molecular composition). A century later, James Watson and Francis Crick discovered the structure of DNA, and the cellular disease became, more precisely, a disease of genes inside cells. Half a century after that, the Human Genome Project finished, and the disease of genes inside cells became readable letter by letter.

The institutional history runs in parallel. In 1937, the United States Congress created the National Cancer Institute — a federal agency dedicated specifically to cancer research. In 1971, Richard Nixon signed the National Cancer Act, declaring what was sometimes called the "War on Cancer" and dramatically expanding federal funding. The war metaphor has aged poorly — cancer is not a single enemy, and "war" implies victories of a kind cancer rarely permits — but the funding was real, and the science it bought is the reason we now have a molecular understanding of the disease at all.

The story is one of slow, accumulating insight rather than dramatic breakthrough. From a papyrus saying *there is no treatment* to a 2026 oncologist saying *here is the specific mutation in your tumor and here is the drug that targets it* is about thirty-five hundred years. The pace looks frustrating when you stand inside it. It looks remarkable when you step back.

## The hallmarks

In 2000, Douglas Hanahan and Robert Weinberg published a paper in *Cell* called "The Hallmarks of Cancer." It is one of the most cited papers in biomedicine, and the reason is simple: it took a sprawling, heterogeneous family of diseases and identified the small number of capabilities that all of them must acquire to become cancer.

I want you to see the move they made. By 2000, cancer biology had accumulated thousands of findings — mutations in this gene drive this kind of leukemia, deletions of that gene drive that kind of sarcoma, methylation here, amplification there. The field was drowning in particulars. Hanahan and Weinberg asked a different kind of question: regardless of which mutations cause it, what does *every* cancer cell need to be able to do?

Their original answer was six things.

A cancer cell must **sustain proliferative signaling**. Normal cells divide only when they receive a specific growth-factor signal from outside. Cancer cells manufacture their own signals, or they have mutated their receptors so the signals look permanently "on" even when there is no growth factor present. Either way, the cell is divorced from the external on/off switch.

A cancer cell must **evade growth suppressors**. Normal cells have built-in brakes — the tumor suppressor genes — that stop division when something is wrong. Cancer cells lose those brakes. p53 is mutated in roughly half of all human cancers, Rb in retinoblastoma and many adult cancers, APC in nearly every colorectal cancer.

A cancer cell must **resist cell death**. Normal cells have a built-in self-destruct mechanism — apoptosis — that fires when DNA damage is severe, when the cell is in the wrong location, or when growth signals are absent. Cancer cells silence the apoptosis program, usually by overexpressing survival proteins like Bcl-2 or by losing p53's death-inducing capacity.

A cancer cell must **enable replicative immortality**. Normal cells count their divisions. Each time a chromosome is copied, the telomeres at its ends shorten slightly; after roughly fifty divisions, the telomeres become too short to protect the chromosome ends, and the cell stops dividing. This is the Hayflick limit. Cancer cells reactivate telomerase, the enzyme that rebuilds telomeres, and the divisional counter is reset. The cell becomes, in principle, immortal.

A cancer cell must **induce angiogenesis**. A tumor of more than about a millimeter cannot survive on diffusion alone. It needs blood vessels. Cancer cells secrete growth factors — VEGF principally — that recruit nearby capillaries to grow into the tumor mass. The new vessels are usually disorganized and leaky, which is part of why tumors look the way they look on imaging and why drugs that block VEGF (bevacizumab is the prototype) extend life in certain cancers.

A cancer cell must **activate invasion and metastasis**. A localized tumor, even an aggressive one, can usually be cut out. What kills patients is the spread — cells that detach from the primary tumor, enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system, lodge in a distant organ, and start growing there. The capacities involved in invasion and metastasis are the ones that distinguish a curable disease from an incurable one in most solid tumors.

In 2011, Hanahan and Weinberg updated the framework. They added two "emerging hallmarks" — **reprogramming energy metabolism** (the Warburg effect; Chapter 11) and **evading immune destruction** (the foundation for the immunotherapy revolution; Chapter 18) — and two "enabling characteristics" — **genome instability and mutation** (which makes everything else possible; Chapter 4) and **tumor-promoting inflammation** (the role of the immune system in some cancers' growth, not just in opposing them).

In 2022, they updated again. Four more "dimensions" were added: **unlocking phenotypic plasticity** (cancer cells dedifferentiating and acquiring new capabilities), **non-mutational epigenetic reprogramming** (changes to chromatin without changes to DNA sequence — Chapter 7), **polymorphic microbiomes** (the gut bacteria of cancer patients differ from those of healthy people, and the difference matters), and **senescent cells** (cells that have stopped dividing but are still secreting factors that affect their neighbors).

The 2022 framework is more complicated than the 2000 one, and that is honest. The field has learned more. Watch the move, though — twenty-two years later, the framework is *still* a finite list of capabilities. Not a list of all the molecular details. Just the things a cancer cell has to be able to do. Each chapter of this book is, in one way or another, the story of one of these hallmarks: how cells acquire that capability, what protein-level changes underlie it, and what therapeutic approaches target it.

## Benign and malignant

Two words you will see in every pathology report. They are not interchangeable.

A **benign** tumor is a mass of cells dividing more than they should but staying where they are. A lipoma — a benign growth of fat cells under the skin — is the friendly example. It gets bigger; it does not invade surrounding tissue; it does not spread to distant organs. If you remove it, it does not come back unless the surgeon left some cells behind. Benign tumors can be life-threatening if they are in the wrong place — a benign meningioma growing against the brainstem will kill the patient by mass effect — but the cells themselves are not behaving in a way that would let them escape and seed elsewhere.

A **malignant** tumor is what people usually mean when they say cancer. Malignant cells invade surrounding tissue, can break off and travel through the bloodstream or lymphatic system, and can lodge in distant organs and grow there. A malignant breast tumor that has spread to the bones is no longer really a breast tumor in the topological sense — but it is genetically and behaviorally a breast tumor, and it is treated as one.

The line between the two is not always crisp. Some benign tumors carry mutations that put them at elevated risk of transforming into malignant tumors. Colorectal polyps are the canonical example: a small adenomatous polyp in the colon is benign, but its cells carry an APC mutation, and over a decade or two the lineage can accumulate enough additional mutations to become a colorectal carcinoma. The colonoscopy procedure works because that lineage is visible and removable for years before it becomes dangerous.

Cancer also gets classified by its tissue of origin.

**Carcinomas** arise from epithelial cells — the cells that line surfaces (skin, gut lining, lung lining) and form the secretory organs (breast, prostate, pancreas). Around 85 to 90 percent of all human cancers are carcinomas. Within carcinomas, *adenocarcinomas* come from glandular epithelium (breast, colon, prostate, lung) and *squamous cell carcinomas* come from flat surface epithelium (skin, cervix, esophagus, parts of the lung).

**Sarcomas** arise from connective tissue — bone, muscle, fat, cartilage, blood vessels. They are rare, perhaps 1 percent of adult cancers, but disproportionately common in children. Osteosarcoma. Rhabdomyosarcoma. Liposarcoma.

**Leukemias and lymphomas** arise from the blood-forming and immune lineages. Leukemias are cancers of bone marrow cells circulating in the blood; lymphomas are cancers of lymphocytes that form solid masses in lymph nodes or organs. Acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL) is the most common childhood cancer. Non-Hodgkin lymphoma is one of the most common adult blood cancers.

**Central nervous system tumors** — gliomas, glioblastomas, medulloblastomas — arise from the cells of the brain and spinal cord. They are biologically distinct enough to warrant their own category.

**Germ cell tumors** arise from the cells that make egg and sperm. Testicular cancer and ovarian germ cell tumors are the main examples.

The point of the taxonomy is not that the categories are pure. They are not. A small-cell lung carcinoma and a non-small-cell lung carcinoma share a lung but are biologically and clinically different diseases. A pancreatic adenocarcinoma is a different problem from a pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor even though both grow in the pancreas. The taxonomy is useful because it tells you, on first encounter, where to look in the textbook — what tissue, what cell type, what likely behavior. The details come later.

## Grade and stage

Two more words. They sound similar. They mean different things.

**Grade** is about how the tumor cells *look*. A pathologist looks at a thin slice of tumor under a microscope and asks: *how much do these cells still resemble the tissue they came from?* A well-differentiated tumor still looks recognizably like glandular epithelium, or like skeletal muscle, or like whatever it descended from. A poorly differentiated tumor is a mass of cells that have lost most of their original identity — bigger nuclei than they should have, irregular shape, no organized structure. Poorly differentiated tumors generally behave more aggressively. Higher grade = worse prognosis, all else equal.

**Stage** is about where the tumor *is*. Has it spread beyond its tissue of origin? Has it reached nearby lymph nodes? Has it metastasized to distant organs? Staging is what determines treatment more than anything else.

The system most commonly used is **TNM**, developed by Pierre Denoix in the 1940s and standardized by the American Joint Committee on Cancer.

**T** is for *tumor*: how big is the primary mass, and how deeply has it invaded? Subscripts go from T1 (small, contained) through T4 (large or invading nearby structures).

**N** is for *nodes*: are nearby lymph nodes involved, and if so, how many? N0 means none. N1, N2, N3 mean increasing nodal involvement.

**M** is for *metastasis*: are there distant deposits? M0 means no. M1 means yes.

These three letters combine into stage groupings — Stage 0 (in situ, hasn't even broken through the basement membrane), Stage I (small primary, no nodes, no mets), Stage II (larger or some local invasion), Stage III (significant local invasion or substantial nodal involvement), Stage IV (distant metastasis present). The five-year survival rate drops dramatically from Stage I to Stage IV across nearly every cancer type. For colorectal cancer it is roughly 90 percent at Stage I and 14 percent at Stage IV. For breast cancer it is roughly 99 percent at Stage I and 30 percent at Stage IV. The numbers vary by cancer, but the pattern is universal: catching cancer early matters more than almost anything else medicine can do for it.

This is why screening exists. A colonoscopy that finds and removes a Stage 0 adenoma prevents a Stage III colorectal cancer ten years later. A mammogram that finds a Stage I breast tumor is the difference between a lumpectomy and a metastatic disease. A low-dose CT scan that finds a Stage I lung nodule in a heavy smoker is the difference between surgery and palliative chemotherapy. Screening is not glamorous. It is not a cure. It is the most powerful intervention oncology has, by a wide margin, because it converts intractable cancers into tractable ones.

There is a trade-off, of course. Screening too aggressively catches cancers that would never have killed the patient — *overdiagnosis* — and exposes people to surgery and radiation and anxiety for tumors that would have stayed indolent. The PSA test for prostate cancer is the canonical case. Some men benefit. Many men are treated for cancers that would have remained quiet for decades. Designing screening programs is a public-health optimization problem with real costs on both sides, and the optimum is different for each cancer and each population.

## How cancer spreads

Metastasis is what kills most cancer patients. A localized primary tumor is usually curable by surgery, radiation, or local chemotherapy. Metastatic disease — cancer that has set up colonies in distant organs — usually is not.

Why some cancer cells metastasize and others do not is one of the deep questions in oncology. The capacity is not trivial to acquire. A cell that detaches from its tumor has to survive several harsh transitions. It has to lose its attachment to the basement membrane (which normally triggers a kind of apoptosis called *anoikis*). It has to squeeze through layers of extracellular matrix (which requires expressing matrix-degrading enzymes — matrix metalloproteinases). It has to enter the bloodstream or lymph (intravasation), survive shear forces and immune attack while circulating, exit into a new tissue (extravasation), and — the hard part — find a microenvironment that will let it grow.

Most cancer cells that detach do not survive. Stephen Paget articulated the metaphor in 1889: cancer cells are *seeds*, distant tissues are *soils*, and only certain seed-soil combinations grow. The biology of why is still being unpacked. Breast cancer preferentially metastasizes to bone, lung, liver, and brain. Colon cancer preferentially to liver and lung. Prostate cancer to bone. The patterns reflect both physical routing (the venous drainage of the colon goes through the liver first; the prostate's drainage goes through the vertebral plexus to the spine) and biological compatibility (some tissues secrete factors that certain cancers respond to; others do not).

The clinical implications are large. A patient diagnosed with prostate cancer is screened for bone metastases because that is where prostate cancer goes. A patient with breast cancer is screened for liver and bone metastases because that is where breast cancer goes. A workup for a patient with cancer of unknown primary — a metastatic tumor whose tissue of origin is not obvious — uses the pattern of metastasis as a clue to where to look.

This is what kills people. A breast tumor in the breast can be removed. A breast tumor that has seeded bone marrow throughout the skeleton, or formed micro-deposits in the lungs and liver, cannot be cut out — not because the surgeon lacks skill but because the disease is no longer localized. Systemic therapy (chemotherapy, hormonal therapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy) is what is left, and systemic therapy is by definition therapy that affects normal tissues too. Chapter 13 traces the cellular and molecular biology of metastasis in detail.

## The numbers

Globally, in 2022, there were approximately 20 million new cancer diagnoses and 9.7 million cancer deaths. These are the most recent comprehensive estimates from GLOBOCAN, the World Health Organization's global cancer surveillance project. The numbers are growing — partly because the world's population is growing and aging, partly because in many countries detection has improved, partly because lifestyle factors (tobacco, obesity, alcohol, processed food) are reshaping cancer incidence in low- and middle-income countries that previously had different disease profiles.

By cancer type, the top five worldwide in 2022 (by new cases) were lung, breast, colorectal, prostate, and stomach. Lung cancer was the leading cause of cancer death — about 1.8 million deaths globally in 2022 — driven primarily by tobacco use, though indoor air pollution and occupational exposures matter too. Breast cancer was the most common cancer in women (2.3 million new cases). Prostate cancer was the most common in men in many high-income countries. Stomach cancer, once the leading cancer worldwide, has declined as refrigeration replaced salt-preservation and as *H. pylori* infection has become treatable.

By geographic distribution, the patterns are striking. High-income countries have higher rates of colorectal, breast, and prostate cancer — partly because of detection, partly because of lifestyle factors associated with industrial diets and sedentary work. Low- and middle-income countries have higher relative rates of cancers caused by infections — cervical cancer (HPV), liver cancer (hepatitis B and C, aflatoxin), stomach cancer (*H. pylori*). About 13 percent of all cancers globally are attributable to infectious agents, and most of those cases occur in countries with limited public-health infrastructure.

In the United States, the American Cancer Society's annual report estimates roughly 2 million new cancer cases and around 600,000 cancer deaths in a typical recent year. Cancer is the second leading cause of death, after heart disease. About 40 percent of Americans will be diagnosed with cancer at some point in their lifetime. About 20 percent will die of it.

These numbers are large and concrete. They are also misleading if not contextualized. Cancer is overwhelmingly a disease of age. Half of all cancer diagnoses occur in people over 65. The lifetime risk reflects the fact that cells accumulate mutations over decades, and that organs accumulate decades' worth of insults. Childhood cancers exist and are devastating, but they are biologically a different problem — different mutations, different cells of origin, different therapeutic landscapes. Most childhood cancers (acute lymphoblastic leukemia is the canonical case) have dramatically better survival rates than adult cancers, in part because they tend to be driven by single dominant mutations that respond cleanly to targeted chemotherapy.

The disparities matter too. Within the United States, Black Americans have higher cancer mortality rates than white Americans across most cancer types, even though their incidence rates are not always higher. Some of this is biology (Black men have higher rates of aggressive prostate cancer, for reasons that include but are not limited to genetic ancestry). Most of it is access — to screening, to specialty care, to early diagnosis, to good insurance, to time off work for treatment. Cancer is not just a biological problem. It is a socioeconomic one, and ignoring that fact mismeasures both the disease and the medicine.

## What cancer is

Hold the picture from Chapter 1 — the normal cell with its organelles, its four-phase cycle, its information system, its signaling networks, its apoptosis program, its tissue context. Cancer is the failure of that picture in coordinated ways. Not one failure. Many failures, compounded.

Cancer is hallmarked by what it does, not by what causes it. The same set of capabilities — sustained proliferation, evaded brakes, resisted death, immortality, angiogenesis, invasion — can arise from radically different starting mutations in radically different tissues. A BRCA1-mutated breast tumor and an EGFR-mutated lung tumor and a Philadelphia-chromosome leukemia look nothing alike at the molecular level, but they have all acquired some subset of the same six-to-ten capabilities. The taxonomy of cancer by tissue (carcinoma, sarcoma, leukemia, lymphoma) is useful for clinic. The taxonomy by hallmark — what the cell can now do that it could not before — is useful for biology.

Cancer is graded and staged. Grade tells you what the cells look like under a microscope. Stage tells you how far they have spread. Stage matters more for prognosis. Both are summarized in the pathology report.

Cancer is a disease of accumulated change. Most cancers take years to develop. Most carry between two and a few hundred mutations that matter, on top of thousands that do not. The mutations accumulate. The cell drifts. Eventually one lineage in one tissue is doing what it wants instead of what the tissue needs.

Cancer is, finally, a problem of dual identity. The cancer cell is your own cell. Therapy that kills it well also tends to damage normal cells. The art of oncology — the search for *therapeutic index*, the difference between the dose that kills cancer and the dose that kills the patient — is the art of finding the small molecular differences that let you hit one without hitting the other. The targeted therapies of the last twenty-five years are the result of finally being able to name those differences at the molecular level. The immunotherapies of the last ten years are the result of teaching the immune system to recognize them.

The rest of this book is the catalog of mechanisms. Each chapter takes one of the failure modes and traces what is broken, how it is broken, and what — if anything — we can do to put it back.
