# Chapter 3 — Cancer Epidemiology and Risk Factors


## TL;DR

- That sentence will make some epidemiologists uneasy and others nod.
- The chapter moves through The global picture, Genes and environment, Tobacco, Alcohol, and related ideas.
- Read it for the main argument, the vocabulary it introduces, and the practical judgment it asks you to develop.

Most cancers are not bad luck.

That sentence will make some epidemiologists uneasy and others nod. The honest version is that some cancers are bad luck, some are environment, some are genes, most are some combination — and the ratios matter for what we can do. Tobacco causes roughly 90 percent of lung cancers. UV radiation causes roughly 90 percent of skin cancers. Obesity, alcohol, and processed food are heavily implicated in colorectal, breast, liver, and pancreatic cancers. About 13 percent of cancers worldwide are caused by infectious agents. A small but biologically important fraction — around 5 to 10 percent — are driven by inherited high-penetrance mutations.

The point of epidemiology is not to assign blame to patients. People do not get cancer because they are bad. People get cancer because cells accumulate mutations, and the rate of mutation accumulation depends on what the cells are exposed to. Some exposures are voluntary. Some are not. Some are structurally imposed — by where you live, by what work you do, by what your skin color signals to a healthcare system, by what foods are available within walking distance.

What epidemiology gives you is the prior probability. *Given that this person is a 65-year-old male with a 40 pack-year smoking history and works around asbestos*, what is the chance their lung mass is cancer? The prior shapes the workup, the screening recommendations, the policy advice, the prevention messaging. It also shapes the questions a biologist asks. The Bradford Hill criteria — strength, consistency, specificity, temporality, biological gradient, plausibility, coherence, experiment, analogy — are the rules epidemiologists use to decide whether an observed association is causal. We will use them implicitly throughout. A finding that smoking correlates with lung cancer in twenty independent studies, with a 20-fold relative risk, with a dose-response gradient, with a biologically plausible mechanism (carcinogens in tar bind DNA and create mutations), with the temporal sequence right (smoking precedes cancer by decades), and with a clean experiment (animals exposed to tobacco smoke get lung cancer) is not just an association. It is a cause.

This chapter does four things. It shows you the global numbers — how cancer incidence and mortality are distributed across the planet. It separates the contribution of genes from the contribution of environment, and shows why the dichotomy is misleading. It walks through the major modifiable risk factors — tobacco, alcohol, obesity, diet, infections, radiation, occupational exposures. And it names the structural disparities that make cancer more common and more lethal in some populations than in others.

Hold the question that organizes the whole chapter: *given a cell, what makes it more likely to acquire the mutations that lead to cancer?* Everything else follows.

## The global picture

In 2022, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) — the cancer arm of the World Health Organization — estimated 20 million new cancer diagnoses and 9.7 million cancer deaths worldwide. The estimates come from the GLOBOCAN database, which compiles cancer registry data from over 185 countries. They are the best numbers we have. They are also imperfect. Cancer registries are well-funded and comprehensive in high-income countries and sparse or absent in many low- and middle-income countries. The numbers from rich countries are within a few percent of truth. The numbers from poor countries are best-effort extrapolations.

The top five cancers by incidence in 2022 were lung, breast, colorectal, prostate, and stomach. Lung was also the top killer — about 1.8 million deaths globally. The leading cancer in women was breast (2.3 million new cases). In men in many high-income countries, the leading cancer was prostate. The pattern shifts dramatically by geography. In sub-Saharan Africa, cervical cancer — caused almost entirely by HPV infection — is still a leading cause of cancer death in women, because HPV vaccination and Pap smear screening are not yet widely available. In East Asia, liver cancer rates are elevated because of hepatitis B and aflatoxin exposure. In the United States and Western Europe, lung cancer rates have been declining for decades as smoking rates have dropped, but obesity-related cancers (colorectal, postmenopausal breast, endometrial, pancreatic) are rising.

What is striking is that the *kinds* of cancer track lifestyle and infection patterns more closely than they track genetics. Within a single ethnic group, immigrating from a low-incidence region to a high-incidence region brings cancer rates up to the new region's pattern within one or two generations. Japanese women in Japan have low rates of breast cancer. Japanese-American women in the United States have nearly the same breast cancer rates as European-American women. The genes did not change. The environment did. (And by "environment" the epidemiologist means everything outside the genome — diet, behavior, exposures, healthcare, culture, structure.)

Cancer is also overwhelmingly a disease of age. The probability that any given cell in your body will accumulate enough mutations to become cancerous in a year is small. The probability that *some* cell, somewhere in your body, will do so over 70 years is much higher. Roughly half of cancer diagnoses occur after age 65. The risk doubles approximately every 15 years through middle age. This is one reason cancer incidence is rising globally — populations are aging — and one reason a "war on cancer" framing oversimplifies. As long as people live long enough, some cells will accumulate the wrong mutations. You can shift the curve. You cannot eliminate the disease.

## Genes and environment

The dichotomy *genes versus environment* is the cleanest place in epidemiology to find a misleading question. The honest answer is *both, and they interact*.

Around 5 to 10 percent of cancers are caused primarily by inherited high-penetrance mutations. *High-penetrance* means: if you have the mutation, your lifetime risk of cancer is dramatically elevated — typically 50 to 90 percent for the specific cancer types associated with the gene. *BRCA1* and *BRCA2* are the most famous examples. A woman with a pathogenic *BRCA1* variant has a lifetime breast cancer risk of about 65 to 80 percent and a lifetime ovarian cancer risk of about 40 to 60 percent, depending on the specific mutation and other factors. *TP53* (Li-Fraumeni syndrome), *APC* (familial adenomatous polyposis), *MLH1*, *MSH2*, *MSH6*, *PMS2* (Lynch syndrome), *VHL* (von Hippel-Lindau), *RB1* (retinoblastoma), *PTEN* (Cowden syndrome) — these are all high-penetrance cancer susceptibility genes. They run in families. They are passed down. They can be tested for.

The next layer is moderate-penetrance variants. *CHEK2*, *PALB2*, *ATM*, *BARD1*, *BRIP1* — these confer cancer risk that is meaningfully elevated above the population baseline but is not the 50-to-90-percent risk of *BRCA1*. A pathogenic *CHEK2* variant roughly doubles a woman's breast cancer risk, from around 12 percent in the general population to around 25 percent. That is not 80 percent, but it is enough to change screening recommendations.

The deepest layer is *low-penetrance variants*. The genome-wide association studies (GWAS) of the last two decades have identified hundreds of common single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) — variants that millions of people carry — each of which slightly raises or lowers cancer risk. Each variant has a small effect (relative risk of 1.05 to 1.20 is typical). Combined, they form a *polygenic risk score* that can identify people whose baseline cancer risk is substantially higher or lower than average. Polygenic scores are now being incorporated into screening guidelines for some cancers. They are not yet definitive enough for most clinical decisions.

The other 90 to 95 percent of cancers are not driven primarily by inherited mutations. The mutations that cause them are *somatic* — acquired in cells over a lifetime, usually because of environmental exposure. The classic case is smoking. A smoker's lung cells accumulate dozens to hundreds of mutations in carcinogen-binding patterns characteristic of tobacco. A lifelong nonsmoker's lung cells accumulate far fewer. The same logic applies to UV exposure and melanoma, hepatitis-induced liver cancer, aflatoxin-induced liver cancer, HPV-induced cervical and oropharyngeal cancer. Carcinogen exposure leaves a *mutational signature* — a recognizable pattern of which bases changed to which other bases at which sequence contexts — and modern cancer genomics can often read those signatures out of a tumor and reconstruct what the cell was exposed to.

The interaction matters. A person carrying a *BRCA1* mutation who never gets pregnant, who uses estrogen replacement therapy, and who drinks heavily has a different cancer trajectory from a *BRCA1* carrier who has children at 22, breastfeeds, and abstains from alcohol. The germline mutation sets the stage. The environment fires the gun. Or does not.

## Tobacco

If you read only one paragraph about cancer prevention, read this one. Tobacco use causes about 22 percent of cancer deaths worldwide and roughly 30 percent in the United States. Lung cancer is the canonical case — smokers are about twenty times more likely to develop lung cancer than nonsmokers — but tobacco causes cancers throughout the body: oral cavity, pharynx, larynx, esophagus, stomach, pancreas, kidney, bladder, cervix, and acute myeloid leukemia. Quitting at any age reduces risk. Quitting before 40 returns lung cancer risk to nearly that of a never-smoker. Quitting at 60 still cuts the risk substantially over the next decade.

Tobacco is the largest preventable cause of cancer death in history. The chemistry of why is unsubtle. Tobacco smoke contains over 70 known human carcinogens — benzo[a]pyrene, N-nitrosamines, polonium-210, formaldehyde, aromatic amines, acetaldehyde, and others. These molecules enter the bloodstream through the lungs, distribute throughout the body, and bind covalently to DNA, forming adducts. The adducts distort the DNA helix in ways that cause replication errors. The errors accumulate over decades. Eventually, in some cell somewhere, the errors converge on the genes that matter — typically *TP53*, *KRAS*, *EGFR*, *CDKN2A* in lung cancer — and the lineage transforms.

The dose-response is steep and clean. A heavy smoker (more than 20 cigarettes a day, more than 30 years) has approximately a 20-fold elevated risk of lung cancer. A moderate smoker has a 10-fold elevated risk. A light smoker has a 3-to-5-fold elevated risk. Even passive smoke exposure (secondhand smoke) raises lung cancer risk by about 25 percent. These numbers come from massive cohort studies — the British Doctors Study (1951-2001), the American Cancer Society's Cancer Prevention Studies, the Whitehall Studies. They are among the most replicated findings in epidemiology.

Smokeless tobacco — chew, snuff, snus — is not safe. It causes cancers of the mouth, esophagus, and pancreas. Smoking pipes and cigars carries elevated cancer risk relative to nonsmoking, though typically lower than cigarettes (which is partly behavioral — pipe and cigar smokers inhale less deeply). E-cigarettes and vaping are newer; the long-term cancer risk is still being characterized. The vapor contains fewer of the classic tobacco carcinogens than cigarette smoke, but it is not zero, and the products and additives vary widely. The honest summary is that we do not yet have 30 years of cohort data on vaping. The early signals are that vaping is less carcinogenic than smoking and not as safe as breathing air. How not-as-safe is being worked out.

## Alcohol

Alcohol causes about 4 percent of cancers globally and is implicated in cancers of the mouth, throat, esophagus, liver, breast, and colon. The mechanism is partly direct — ethanol is metabolized to acetaldehyde, a known carcinogen that binds DNA — and partly indirect (alcohol contributes to obesity, alters nutrient absorption, increases estrogen, and damages liver tissue, setting the stage for hepatocellular carcinoma).

For breast cancer, even moderate drinking matters. A meta-analysis of over 100 studies found that each daily drink raises breast cancer risk by about 7 to 10 percent. A woman who has two drinks a day has roughly a 30 to 50 percent higher relative risk of breast cancer than a non-drinker. The absolute increase is small in any single year, but it accumulates over a lifetime. This is one of the findings that the public health message "one drink a day is good for your heart" has badly underweighted. Cardiovascular benefits of moderate alcohol consumption have been increasingly questioned in the past decade; the cancer harms are unambiguous.

For liver cancer, the relationship is more dose-dependent and tied to chronic damage. Heavy alcohol use causes cirrhosis. Cirrhotic livers — scarred, regenerating, full of inflammation and oxidative damage — are fertile soil for hepatocellular carcinoma. Hepatitis B and C, separately and in combination with alcohol, accelerate the process. In sub-Saharan Africa and parts of East Asia, aflatoxin contamination of stored grains compounds the risk further.

## Obesity and metabolic factors

Obesity is now causally linked to at least 13 different cancers — including colorectal, postmenopausal breast, endometrial, esophageal (adenocarcinoma), gastric (cardia), pancreatic, kidney, gallbladder, liver, thyroid, ovarian, multiple myeloma, and meningioma. The mechanisms are mechanistic and overlapping. Adipose tissue is not inert; it is an endocrine organ. Excess body fat raises circulating estrogen (which drives some breast and endometrial cancers), elevates insulin and insulin-like growth factor 1 (which signal through pathways that promote cell proliferation), and creates a chronic low-grade inflammatory state (which promotes mutation accumulation and tumor microenvironment changes that favor cancer growth).

In high-income countries, obesity is now the second most important modifiable cancer risk factor after tobacco. As smoking rates have fallen, obesity rates have risen, and the relative contribution of obesity to cancer mortality has been rising. The estimated attributable fraction of cancer cases linked to excess body weight in the United States is around 8 percent and growing. Worldwide, the WHO estimates that obesity contributes to about 4 to 8 percent of cancers, with substantial variation by region.

What makes obesity epidemiologically difficult is that it is downstream of so many other things — diet quality, physical activity, sleep, stress, food access, food economics, the marketing budget of ultra-processed food companies, the design of cities that make walking optional. Treating obesity as a personal lifestyle choice misses most of the causal structure. The cancer epidemiology is clear regardless of the upstream attribution: more body fat over more years correlates with higher cancer risk in specific tissues, and reducing body fat (through whatever means works) reduces that risk.

## Diet and physical activity

Diet beyond obesity is harder to study and easier to oversimplify. Some findings are robust. *Processed meat* — bacon, sausage, ham, hot dogs — has been classified by IARC as Group 1 carcinogenic to humans, primarily for colorectal cancer. The estimated relative risk increase is modest (about 17 percent per 50 grams of processed meat per day), but the classification is on the basis of the underlying mechanism (N-nitroso compounds, heterocyclic amines, heme iron) and the consistency of findings. *Red meat* — unprocessed beef, pork, lamb — is classified as Group 2A (probably carcinogenic). High intake of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and fiber is consistently associated with lower colorectal cancer risk. Mediterranean and DASH-style diets are associated with lower overall cancer mortality.

Beyond those, the diet literature gets messier. Studies of specific micronutrients have produced inconsistent results. Beta-carotene was a leading anti-cancer candidate in the 1980s based on observational data; large randomized trials (the ATBC and CARET trials) found that beta-carotene supplementation in smokers actually *increased* lung cancer risk, possibly because of pro-oxidant effects at supraphysiologic doses. Selenium supplementation appeared protective in some studies and harmful in others. Vitamin D has been studied extensively with mixed results. The honest summary is that whole-food dietary patterns matter; individual nutrient supplements rarely do.

Physical activity reduces cancer risk through multiple pathways. Higher activity correlates with lower colorectal, postmenopausal breast, endometrial, kidney, esophageal, and bladder cancer rates. The relative risk reduction is modest in any individual study (typically 10 to 30 percent for the most active versus the least active) but consistent. The mechanisms involve weight regulation, reduced inflammation, improved insulin sensitivity, modulation of circulating sex hormones, and direct effects on immune surveillance.

## Infections

About 13 percent of cancers worldwide are caused by infectious agents. The list is short and consequential.

**Human papillomavirus (HPV)** causes essentially all cervical cancer, a substantial fraction of oropharyngeal cancers, and most anal cancers. Two specific high-risk types (HPV-16 and HPV-18) account for the majority. The HPV vaccine — Gardasil (originally a quadrivalent vaccine; the current 9-valent version covers nine HPV types) — prevents the initial infection in vaccinated populations. Australia, which implemented universal HPV vaccination for adolescents in 2007, is on track to effectively eliminate cervical cancer in coming decades. The biology is one of the cleanest cancer prevention stories ever written.

**Hepatitis B and C** cause hepatocellular carcinoma. Hepatitis B is preventable by vaccination (the first cancer-preventing vaccine, in widespread use since the 1980s). Hepatitis C is now curable with direct-acting antiviral therapy in most cases. Liver cancer rates have declined dramatically in countries with universal hepatitis B vaccination and aggressive hepatitis C treatment.

***Helicobacter pylori*** causes the majority of stomach cancers and most gastric MALT lymphomas. The bacterium colonizes the stomach lining, induces chronic inflammation, and over decades drives progression from gastritis through atrophic gastritis and intestinal metaplasia to gastric adenocarcinoma. *H. pylori* is curable with a one-to-two-week course of antibiotics. Population-level eradication programs in high-incidence countries (Japan, Korea, parts of Latin America) are reducing stomach cancer rates.

**Epstein-Barr virus** is implicated in Burkitt lymphoma (especially in equatorial Africa), nasopharyngeal carcinoma (especially in East Asia), Hodgkin lymphoma, and some gastric cancers.

**Human T-cell lymphotropic virus type 1 (HTLV-1)** causes adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma. Endemic in parts of Japan and the Caribbean.

**HIV** does not cause cancer directly but enables several cancers (Kaposi sarcoma, non-Hodgkin lymphoma, cervical cancer) by suppressing immune surveillance.

The infection-to-cancer relationships matter for two reasons. Biologically, they show how viral oncoproteins (HPV E6/E7 targeting p53 and Rb; hepatitis B X protein; EBV LMP1) provide windows into normal tumor-suppressor function. Practically, they are *prevention targets*. Vaccination, screening, and antimicrobial therapy can prevent a substantial fraction of these cancers. The HPV vaccine is the most powerful cancer-prevention intervention developed in the past half century. Universal hepatitis B vaccination is the second.

## Radiation and the sun

Ionizing radiation — X-rays, gamma rays, alpha and beta particles — damages DNA directly, by ionizing molecules and creating reactive species that break DNA strands. Exposure raises cancer risk roughly linearly with dose. The classic studies are of atomic bomb survivors in Hiroshima and Nagasaki (the Life Span Study), nuclear workers, radon-exposed underground miners, and patients given therapeutic radiation. The relative risks per unit dose are well-characterized; the absolute risks at typical environmental doses are small but real.

Diagnostic medical imaging contributes some radiation exposure. A typical chest X-ray delivers about 0.1 millisievert. A CT scan can deliver 5 to 15 millisieverts. A whole-body CT in a single sitting can deliver 20 millisieverts or more. The cumulative dose matters. Children are more sensitive than adults. Pregnant women's fetuses are especially sensitive. The clinical principle is to use imaging when it changes management and to use the lowest dose that produces an interpretable image — *as low as reasonably achievable*.

Radon, a naturally occurring radioactive gas that seeps from soil and rock, is the second leading cause of lung cancer after smoking in the United States. It accumulates in basements. Testing kits are inexpensive. Mitigation, where elevated levels are found, is a one-time installation cost.

Ultraviolet radiation is non-ionizing but still mutagenic. UV-B causes pyrimidine dimers in DNA (the same lesion described in Chapter 1) and is the principal cause of most skin cancers — basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and melanoma. Tanning beds deliver intense UV-A and UV-B and are classified as Group 1 carcinogens. Sunscreen (broad-spectrum, SPF 30 or higher), protective clothing, and avoidance of midday sun exposure are the main preventive measures.

## Occupational and environmental exposures

A long list of chemicals and exposures are known human carcinogens. The IARC's Group 1 list includes asbestos, benzene, vinyl chloride, formaldehyde, chromium VI, nickel compounds, beryllium, cadmium, arsenic, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (in soot, coal tar, diesel exhaust), and dozens more. Most of these were identified as carcinogens through occupational epidemiology — workers in specific industries developed specific cancers at rates much higher than the general population, and the identification of the responsible exposure led to regulation.

Asbestos and mesothelioma is the canonical case. Asbestos fibers, inhaled and lodged in the pleura (the lining of the lung), cause an otherwise rare cancer of the pleural lining. Latency from exposure to diagnosis is typically 30 to 40 years. Mesothelioma was vanishingly rare before industrial asbestos use and became increasingly common in workers in shipbuilding, construction, insulation, and brake-pad manufacture. Recognition of the link in the 1960s and 70s led to bans on most uses of asbestos in high-income countries by the 1980s and 90s, but the long latency means cases are still appearing in workers exposed decades ago. In countries where asbestos is still used (some forms of chrysotile asbestos are still mined and used in lower-income countries), the epidemic is ahead, not behind.

Benzene and acute myeloid leukemia is another canonical case. Workers in petroleum refineries, chemical plants, and (historically) shoe factories developed AML at elevated rates. The carcinogen is benzene itself, metabolized in the liver to reactive intermediates that damage hematopoietic stem cells.

Air pollution — particularly fine particulate matter (PM2.5) — is now an IARC Group 1 carcinogen. Lung cancer rates rise with chronic exposure. The effect is smaller than tobacco's per individual but enormous in population terms, because air pollution affects everyone in a region rather than only smokers. Urban air pollution in high-pollution regions is associated with measurable increases in lung cancer mortality.

## Disparities

Within and between countries, cancer outcomes are unequal. In the United States, Black Americans have higher cancer mortality rates than white Americans for most cancer types. Native Americans have higher mortality for some specific cancers. People living in rural areas have higher mortality than people in urban areas, on average. Uninsured patients have worse outcomes than insured ones. Patients without paid sick leave have worse outcomes than those with it. These patterns are not artifacts of single studies; they are reproduced in registry data across decades.

Some of the disparity is biological. There is real evidence that triple-negative breast cancer — a particularly aggressive subtype — is more common in women of African ancestry. There is real evidence that prostate cancer in Black men is more often a fast-growing, lethal disease rather than the slow-growing variant that screening can largely defer. These biological differences are partly genetic and partly environmental (chronic stress, for instance, has been mechanistically linked to gene expression changes in tumor cells through cortisol signaling).

Most of the disparity is structural. Black Americans are less likely to be screened for cancer at recommended intervals. They are less likely to receive guideline-concordant treatment. They are diagnosed at later stages. They are less likely to be referred to specialized cancer centers. They are less likely to participate in clinical trials. The cumulative effect is that, controlling for stage at diagnosis, Black patients often have worse outcomes — which means the system is delivering different care to different people.

Income matters across all racial groups. Uninsured patients are diagnosed later, treated less aggressively, and die more. Lack of paid sick leave during treatment is associated with worse outcomes for working-class cancer patients. Distance to a comprehensive cancer center matters for survival in rural areas. The structure of the American healthcare system — fragmented, insurance-dependent, unevenly distributed — produces outcomes that are unjust by any reasonable measure.

Globally, the disparities are starker. Low- and middle-income countries account for about 70 percent of global cancer deaths but receive a fraction of the cancer-related research funding and have limited access to the specialty care and pharmaceuticals that drive survival in high-income countries. Childhood cancer survival rates in low-income countries are around 20 percent; in high-income countries they are over 80 percent. The biology is identical. The access is not.

## What this chapter gives you

The risk factors are knowable. The mechanisms are tractable. The interventions exist. Most cancers are preventable in principle — through reducing tobacco use, addressing obesity, vaccinating against HPV and hepatitis B, treating *H. pylori*, screening for early-stage disease, regulating known carcinogens, and addressing the structural inequities that determine who gets screened and treated well.

Most cancers do not happen because of one bad gene. They happen because cells in tissues accumulate mutations over years, and the rate of accumulation depends on what the cells are exposed to and on the body's capacity to repair damage. Some of those exposures are personal. Many are not. The honest medical conversation about cancer risk is at the population level: *given these exposures, given this age, given this family history, given this access to care*, what is the prior probability that this person will develop this cancer in the next decade? The biology of the rest of this book lives downstream of that prior.
