# Chapter 10 — Cancer Etiology: Viral and Bacterial Carcinogens


## TL;DR

- About one in eight human cancers is caused by an infection.
- The chapter moves through How viruses cause cancer, Human papillomavirus, Hepatitis B and C, Epstein-Barr virus, and related ideas.
- Read it for the main argument, the vocabulary it introduces, and the practical judgment it asks you to develop.

About one in eight human cancers is caused by an infection.

Hold the number for a moment. *Twelve percent* of all human cancers — roughly 2.2 million new cases per year worldwide — are caused by microbes. Cervical cancer is essentially all HPV. Hepatocellular carcinoma is dominated by hepatitis B and C. Stomach cancer is *Helicobacter pylori*. Kaposi sarcoma is KSHV. Burkitt lymphoma is Epstein-Barr virus. Adult T-cell leukemia is HTLV-1. The list is finite but consequential, and almost all of it is in principle preventable — through vaccination, antimicrobial therapy, or behavioral interventions.

This matters because infectious carcinogens are unusually tractable. A chemical carcinogen is hard to remove from the environment. A genetic predisposition cannot be edited. But a virus can be vaccinated against. A bacterium can be eradicated with antibiotics. A chronic infection can be cleared with antivirals. The HPV vaccine, in the relatively few countries that have implemented universal vaccination, is on a path to effectively eliminating cervical cancer. The hepatitis B vaccine has dramatically reduced liver cancer rates in vaccinated populations. *H. pylori* eradication programs in high-incidence countries are reducing stomach cancer. The biology of microbial carcinogenesis is, in this sense, the most actionable cancer biology there is.

This chapter — the second on cancer etiology — covers the biological carcinogens. The mechanism by which a microbe causes cancer is different from chemical or radiation carcinogenesis. Viruses can introduce *viral oncoproteins* that directly drive proliferation or block tumor suppression. Chronic bacterial infections can drive cancer through *sustained inflammation* — generating reactive species, recruiting mutagenic immune cells, and altering tissue architecture over decades. Some viruses integrate their DNA into the host genome and disrupt nearby genes. Some persist as episomes (viral DNA circles in the nucleus) without integration. The pathways are diverse but the common element is *persistent infection*. Acute infections that the immune system clears do not cause cancer. Persistent infections — those that establish a long-term, low-level presence — do.

The chapter closes by tying together all the etiologies — chemical, radiation, biological — through the *multifactorial framework* that recognizes most cancers as products of multiple converging exposures, genetic predispositions, and chance.

## How viruses cause cancer

The relationship between viruses and cancer was suggested in 1911 by Peyton Rous (Chapter 5) and largely ignored for fifty years. By the 1960s and 70s, multiple animal tumor viruses had been characterized — and in the 1980s and 90s, human tumor viruses were systematically identified. The IARC has classified seven viruses as Group 1 (carcinogenic to humans): human papillomaviruses (HPV), hepatitis B virus (HBV), hepatitis C virus (HCV), Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), Kaposi sarcoma-associated herpesvirus (KSHV/HHV-8), human T-cell lymphotropic virus type 1 (HTLV-1), and Merkel cell polyomavirus. HIV is classified separately as a Group 1 carcinogen because it enables several cancers through immunosuppression rather than directly transforming cells.

The mechanisms by which viruses cause cancer fall into several patterns.

*Direct expression of viral oncoproteins.* The most direct mechanism. HPV encodes E6 and E7 proteins that bind and degrade p53 and Rb respectively, removing the two most important tumor suppressors. EBV encodes LMP1 and EBNA proteins that drive proliferation and survival. KSHV encodes a viral cyclin, a viral interleukin-6 homolog, and other proteins that mimic cellular oncogenic signaling. HTLV-1 encodes Tax and HBZ proteins that drive T-cell proliferation. The viral oncoproteins are the smoking gun — direct molecular agents of transformation.

*Integration-mediated insertional mutagenesis.* When a virus integrates into the host genome, the integration site can disrupt nearby genes or place them under viral promoter control. HBV integrates into the host genome in most chronic infections and can disrupt tumor suppressors at the integration site. HPV's high-risk strains (HPV-16, HPV-18) frequently integrate during cancer progression, often disrupting the E2 viral gene (which normally regulates E6/E7 expression) and thereby upregulating the oncoproteins.

*Chronic inflammation.* Persistent infection drives chronic immune response. The inflammatory cells generate reactive oxygen and nitrogen species that damage DNA. Repeated cycles of tissue damage and regeneration produce many cell divisions, providing many opportunities for replication errors. This is the dominant mechanism for hepatitis B/C-driven liver cancer and *H. pylori*-driven stomach cancer.

*Immunosuppression.* Some viruses cause cancer indirectly by suppressing immune surveillance, allowing other infections (and the cells they transform) to proliferate. HIV is the canonical example.

The latency between infection and cancer is long. HPV infection typically precedes cervical cancer by 10-20 years. HBV/HCV infection typically precedes hepatocellular carcinoma by 20-40 years. *H. pylori* colonization typically precedes gastric cancer by 30-50 years. The long latency reflects the multistep nature of microbial carcinogenesis — persistent infection, chronic inflammation, accumulating mutations, eventual loss of tumor suppressors, and finally transformation. It also reflects the optimistic side: in the long window between infection and cancer, intervention can interrupt the process.

## Human papillomavirus

Human papillomaviruses are small DNA viruses that infect epithelial cells. About 200 HPV types have been identified, of which roughly 40 infect anogenital sites. The clinically significant types fall into *low-risk* (HPV-6, HPV-11, others) — causing genital warts and benign lesions — and *high-risk* (HPV-16, HPV-18, HPV-31, HPV-45, others) — causing essentially all cervical cancers and a substantial fraction of other cancers.

The virus enters basal epithelial cells through microabrasions. Its lifecycle is tightly coupled to the differentiation program of the squamous epithelium: viral genome replication, capsid assembly, and release happen in differentiating cells as they move toward the surface. Most HPV infections clear spontaneously within 1-2 years; the immune system handles them. Persistent infection — usually defined as detection of the same high-risk type at multiple visits over a year or more — is the precursor state for cancer.

The transforming activity comes from two viral proteins, E6 and E7.

*E6* binds to a cellular ubiquitin ligase, E6AP, and redirects it to ubiquitinate p53. The ubiquitinated p53 is rapidly degraded by the proteasome. The cell loses p53 function. Damaged DNA no longer triggers cell cycle arrest or apoptosis.

*E7* binds Rb (and the related Rb-family proteins p107 and p130) and disrupts the Rb-E2F interaction. E2F is released. S-phase genes are turned on. The cell enters DNA replication regardless of external growth signals.

Together, E6 and E7 do to a cell what mutations in *TP53* and *RB1* would do — except they do it acutely upon infection rather than gradually through mutation accumulation. The basal epithelial cell loses its two most important checkpoints. With persistent E6/E7 expression over years, additional mutations accumulate, and the cell progresses through cervical intraepithelial neoplasia (CIN) grades 1, 2, 3 to invasive carcinoma.

The progression is observable by screening. Pap smears (cytological examination of cervical cells) detect CIN lesions years before invasive cancer. HPV testing (DNA-based detection of high-risk types) is now used as a screening test, alone or combined with cytology. Lesions detected at CIN 2/3 can be removed by colposcopy-guided procedures, preventing progression to cancer. Cervical cancer screening, where it is widely available, has dramatically reduced cervical cancer mortality.

Vaccination is the upstream intervention. The first HPV vaccine, Gardasil, was approved by the FDA in 2006 — a quadrivalent vaccine protecting against HPV-6, 11, 16, and 18. Cervarix followed in 2009 (bivalent, against HPV-16 and 18). Gardasil 9, approved in 2014, covers nine HPV types and prevents approximately 90 percent of cervical cancers. The vaccines work by stimulating antibody responses against the viral capsid protein L1, preventing infection from establishing.

Coverage matters. Australia, which adopted universal HPV vaccination of adolescent girls in 2007 and boys in 2013, has seen HPV prevalence in young women drop dramatically and is on a trajectory to effectively eliminate cervical cancer. The United States has lower coverage — about 60 percent of adolescents are fully vaccinated as of mid-2020s, well short of the 80 percent target — and the resulting cancer-prevention effect is more modest. Low- and middle-income countries, which carry most of the global cervical cancer burden, are still building vaccination infrastructure. The disparity is significant; cervical cancer mortality in sub-Saharan Africa is roughly 10 times higher than in high-income countries.

HPV also causes other cancers. Oropharyngeal cancers (especially of the tonsils and base of the tongue) are now predominantly HPV-driven in much of the developed world — a striking shift over the past three decades, partly as smoking-related oropharyngeal cancers have declined and HPV-related ones have risen. Anal cancers are mostly HPV-driven. Some vulvar, vaginal, and penile cancers are HPV-related. The vaccine, if administered before exposure (typically in early adolescence), prevents all of these.

## Hepatitis B and C

Hepatocellular carcinoma is the third leading cause of cancer death worldwide. Most of it is caused by chronic infection with hepatitis B virus (HBV) or hepatitis C virus (HCV).

*Hepatitis B virus* is a small DNA virus that infects hepatocytes. Acute infection causes a self-limited illness in most adults but becomes chronic in about 5 percent. In children, particularly those infected at birth, chronicity is much more common — 90 percent of perinatally acquired infections become chronic. About 260 million people worldwide are chronic HBV carriers; the majority live in sub-Saharan Africa and East Asia.

The mechanism of HBV-driven cancer is partly indirect (chronic inflammation and regeneration producing accumulating mutations) and partly direct (HBV DNA frequently integrates into the host genome, sometimes disrupting tumor suppressors or oncogenes). The HBx viral protein has weak oncogenic activity, affecting transcription, signaling, and DNA repair. The cumulative effect over decades of chronic infection is a substantially elevated risk of hepatocellular carcinoma — roughly 100-fold compared to uninfected individuals in some populations.

Hepatitis B vaccination is one of the great preventive achievements of modern medicine. The vaccine, introduced in the early 1980s, is a recombinant subunit vaccine that produces lasting immunity. Countries that have implemented universal infant vaccination have seen dramatic drops in chronic HBV infection in vaccinated cohorts and corresponding reductions in childhood hepatocellular carcinoma rates. The WHO has identified HBV vaccination as a priority intervention for cancer prevention; coverage is improving but still uneven.

*Hepatitis C virus* is an RNA virus that, unlike HBV, does not integrate into the host genome. It establishes chronic infection in about 80 percent of acutely infected individuals. About 50 million people worldwide are chronic HCV carriers. Like HBV, the carcinogenesis is largely driven by chronic inflammation, with hepatocellular carcinoma developing typically 20-40 years after infection.

What is dramatic about HCV is that it became *curable* in 2014 with the introduction of direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) — sofosbuvir, ledipasvir, and others. The treatments achieve cure rates above 95 percent in most populations, with relatively short courses (8-12 weeks) and modest side effects. Treatment of chronic HCV reduces hepatocellular carcinoma risk substantially, though does not eliminate it (residual risk persists in patients who had developed cirrhosis before treatment). The DAAs are one of the cleanest examples of a curable cause of cancer in adult medicine.

Aflatoxin and HBV interact synergistically in countries where both are common (sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Southeast Asia). Aflatoxin produces specific *TP53* mutations (R249S, the codon-249 hot spot mentioned in 8A). HBV adds chronic inflammation and additional mutagenesis. The combined effect produces hepatocellular carcinoma at higher rates and earlier ages than either factor alone. Public health interventions that reduce aflatoxin exposure (improved grain storage, education about moldy food) complement HBV vaccination.

## Epstein-Barr virus

Epstein-Barr virus is a herpesvirus that infects more than 90 percent of adults worldwide. Primary infection in early childhood is usually asymptomatic; primary infection in adolescents or young adults often presents as infectious mononucleosis. After acute infection, EBV establishes lifelong latency in B-lymphocytes, where it persists in essentially every infected person without causing disease in most.

In a minority of people, EBV is associated with cancer. The cancers include *Burkitt lymphoma* (especially endemic Burkitt in equatorial Africa, where EBV plus malaria-induced B-cell hyperproliferation drives MYC translocation), *nasopharyngeal carcinoma* (especially in southern China and some other Asian populations), *Hodgkin lymphoma* (subset), *diffuse large B-cell lymphoma in immunocompromised individuals*, *NK/T-cell lymphoma*, and *gastric carcinoma* (about 10 percent of gastric cancers are EBV-positive).

The viral oncogenes are LMP1, LMP2A, EBNA1, EBNA2, and others, varying by latency program. LMP1 in particular mimics constitutively active CD40 signaling, driving NF-κB activation and B-cell proliferation. The transforming activity is potent enough that EBV-infected B-cells can be immortalized in culture — the foundation for many laboratory studies.

The geographic pattern of EBV-associated cancers reflects the interaction of viral biology with cofactors — malaria in Africa, dietary nitrosamines in southern China, immunosuppression in HIV patients and transplant recipients. EBV alone is necessary but not sufficient for most of these cancers; the cofactors matter.

There is no licensed EBV vaccine as of 2026, though several are in clinical development. Treatments for EBV-positive cancers include conventional chemotherapy, immune approaches (EBV-specific T-cell therapies are emerging), and antiviral approaches under investigation.

## Kaposi sarcoma-associated herpesvirus

Kaposi sarcoma-associated herpesvirus (KSHV, also called HHV-8) was identified in 1994 as the cause of Kaposi sarcoma — a vascular tumor that became dramatically common in HIV/AIDS patients during the 1980s and 90s. KSHV is also the cause of primary effusion lymphoma and the lymphoproliferative disorder multicentric Castleman disease.

KSHV is endemic in sub-Saharan Africa, parts of the Mediterranean, and parts of South America. In the developed world, it is largely associated with HIV co-infection. The virus establishes lifelong latency in B-cells and endothelial cells.

The viral genome encodes a remarkable set of oncogenes — a viral cyclin that activates CDK6, a viral interleukin-6 homolog that signals through cellular IL-6 receptors, viral GPCRs that drive constitutive signaling, and others. The viral genome essentially encodes a customized oncogenic toolkit.

Kaposi sarcoma incidence in HIV patients has dropped substantially in countries with widespread access to antiretroviral therapy — restoring immune function suppresses KSHV-driven lesions. This is one of the cleanest examples of how immunosuppression-related cancers respond to immune restoration.

## HTLV-1 and Merkel cell polyomavirus

*HTLV-1* is an RNA retrovirus endemic in Japan (especially southern Kyushu), the Caribbean, parts of West Africa, and parts of South America. About 5 percent of infected individuals develop *adult T-cell leukemia/lymphoma* over a typical latency of 30-60 years. HTLV-1 infects CD4+ T-cells and encodes Tax and HBZ proteins that drive proliferation. The disease is aggressive when it develops; treatment is challenging. Prevention focuses on screening blood products and preventing mother-to-child transmission via breastfeeding.

*Merkel cell polyomavirus* was identified in 2008 as the cause of Merkel cell carcinoma, a rare and aggressive skin cancer. The virus integrates into the host genome and expresses a truncated large T antigen that drives proliferation. About 80 percent of Merkel cell carcinomas carry the viral DNA. The remaining 20 percent are UV-driven (the cancer occurs on sun-exposed skin) and have very high mutational burden — these tumors respond well to immune checkpoint inhibitors, providing one of the cleaner examples of mutational burden predicting immunotherapy response.

## Helicobacter pylori and gastric cancer

*Helicobacter pylori* is a spiral-shaped bacterium that colonizes the human stomach. About half of the world's population carries it. Infection is typically acquired in childhood and persists for life in the absence of treatment. Most infections are asymptomatic; a fraction cause gastritis, peptic ulcers, or — in a small minority — gastric cancer or gastric MALT lymphoma.

The discovery was a famous case of overlooked evidence. Barry Marshall and Robin Warren, working in Perth, Australia, in the early 1980s, isolated *H. pylori* from stomach biopsies and proposed that it caused ulcers and gastritis. The medical establishment was skeptical; the dogma held that no bacterium could survive the stomach's acid and that ulcers were caused by stress and acid. Marshall famously drank a culture of *H. pylori* and gave himself gastritis to demonstrate the causal connection. The work earned him and Warren the 2005 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

The mechanism of *H. pylori*-driven gastric cancer is gradual. Chronic infection causes persistent gastritis. Some strains carry the cag pathogenicity island, including the CagA protein, which is injected into gastric epithelial cells and alters their signaling. The chronic inflammation generates reactive oxygen and nitrogen species that damage DNA. Repeated cycles of damage and repair produce mutations. The gastric epithelium progresses through chronic superficial gastritis, atrophic gastritis (where parietal cells producing acid die), intestinal metaplasia (where the gastric epithelium takes on intestinal-like characteristics), dysplasia, and eventually gastric adenocarcinoma. The full sequence typically takes 30-50 years.

The virulence factors matter. *H. pylori* strains carrying the CagA-positive cag pathogenicity island are associated with higher cancer risk than CagA-negative strains. Geographic variation in strain composition contributes to the geographic variation in gastric cancer incidence — East Asian strains tend to be more virulent than European or African strains.

*H. pylori* eradication therapy — typically a 1-2 week course of a proton pump inhibitor plus two antibiotics (clarithromycin and amoxicillin in standard triple therapy, or alternatives in regions with high clarithromycin resistance) — successfully clears the infection in 80-95 percent of patients. Meta-analyses of long-term follow-up have shown that eradication reduces gastric cancer incidence by approximately 35 percent overall, with stronger effects in patients without pre-existing precancerous changes. Japan, where gastric cancer is among the most common cancers, has implemented a national *H. pylori* eradication program since 2013. The full effect on cancer incidence will play out over decades because of the long latency, but early data are encouraging.

## The multifactorial framework

No real cancer is caused by a single agent acting alone. The accumulated evidence from epidemiology, animal studies, and genomics points to a multifactorial picture in which most cancers are products of multiple converging influences over decades.

Consider a typical case. A 65-year-old develops lung cancer. The patient smoked for 40 years (the dominant initiator). They lived in a region with elevated radon levels (a secondary initiator). They had genetic variants that affect metabolism of tobacco carcinogens (susceptibility modifiers, identified through GWAS). They had a personal history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (an inflammatory promoter). Their lung tumor carries mutations in *TP53*, *KRAS*, *CDKN2A*, *KEAP1*, and a few others (the accumulated drivers). The mutational signature in the tumor genome is dominated by the tobacco-PAH signature (the fingerprint of the dominant exposure). No single one of these is the *cause* — they are all contributing causes, integrated over the patient's life.

The multistep theory of carcinogenesis, articulated by Peter Nowell and refined by Bert Vogelstein and many others, holds that cancer arises through accumulated mutations and clonal selection over decades. The hallmarks of cancer (Hanahan and Weinberg, 2000, 2011, 2022) describe the capabilities a cancer cell must acquire. The carcinogens of this chapter (chemical, radiation, viral, bacterial) describe how those capabilities are typically acquired — through mutation and through chronic inflammation. The susceptibility variants of Chapter 4 describe how some people are more vulnerable to specific exposures than others.

Two enabling characteristics, in Hanahan and Weinberg's framing, are particularly relevant here. *Genomic instability* allows mutations to accumulate faster — and many of the carcinogens of this chapter either directly cause genomic instability (radiation, asbestos) or are facilitated by underlying instability. *Tumor-promoting inflammation* allows clonal expansion of initiated cells — and the chronic infections of this chapter (*H. pylori*, HBV, HCV) act largely through chronic inflammation.

The multifactorial framework also explains *gene-environment interactions*. A *BRCA1* carrier who never gets pregnant, who uses estrogen replacement therapy, and who drinks heavily has a different cancer trajectory than a *BRCA1* carrier who has children early and minimizes other risk factors. The germline mutation sets the stage; the environmental exposures determine whether and how the disease unfolds. Population-level cancer prevention works through environmental modification (smoking cessation, vaccination, screening), but individual cancer risk reflects both genetic and environmental factors integrated over decades.

This is the framework into which the rest of this book fits. Cancer is a clonal evolutionary process driven by mutations from chemical, radiation, and biological exposures, modulated by individual genetic susceptibility, accelerated by chronic inflammation, and selected within tissues over decades. The interventions — primary prevention, screening, targeted therapy, immunotherapy — work at different points in the process. Understanding the mechanisms of each etiology is the foundation for understanding the interventions.

## What this chapter gives you

Biological carcinogens — viruses, bacteria, parasites — cause about 12 percent of human cancers worldwide. The mechanisms involve viral oncoproteins (HPV E6/E7, EBV LMP1, KSHV viral cyclin), viral DNA integration, chronic inflammation driving accumulating mutations (*H. pylori*, HBV, HCV), and immunosuppression (HIV). The latency between infection and cancer is typically decades, providing windows for intervention.

The interventions are tractable and proven. HPV vaccination prevents essentially all cervical cancer if achieved in time. Hepatitis B vaccination prevents most hepatitis-B-driven liver cancer. Hepatitis C treatment with direct-acting antivirals cures the chronic infection. *H. pylori* eradication reduces gastric cancer incidence. The remaining global burden of infection-related cancer is largely a matter of vaccination access, antimicrobial availability, and public health infrastructure.

The multifactorial framework integrates the etiologies of Chapters 3, 4, 8A, and this chapter. Cancer is rarely caused by a single agent; it is the product of converging exposures, susceptibilities, and selection over time. The framework informs both biology (how cancers develop) and policy (where prevention dollars yield the largest return).

Chapter 9 returns to the molecular machinery — the cell cycle and its checkpoints — and traces how the mutations and exposures of these earlier chapters converge on the proliferative engine that cancer hijacks.
